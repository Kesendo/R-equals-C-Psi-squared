## What this document is about

The palindromic mirror Π acts on the full operator space of a multi-qubit system. Can it
be factored into independent per-site operations (Π = Π_A ⊗ Π_B), or is it fundamentally
entangled across sites? We once answered: for all but two of 36 exotic coupling
combinations, Π factors; for the last two (XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY) it does not, and the mirror is
genuinely non-local. That answer was a lens. It was true only as long as the per-site map
was allowed to be a discrete signed permutation. Widen the map to a continuous block
rotation and even those two factor: a single per-site unitary M, the same on every site,
mirrors the whole chain. The mirror is local. This document keeps the original analysis
intact and shows, at the points where it mattered, why the non-local reading was the
shadow of a narrow search rather than a property of the physics.

---

## Abstract

The palindromic spectral symmetry of open quantum systems is generated by a conjugation
operator Π on Liouville space, which for multi-qubit systems has a natural tensor-product
structure across sites. We ask whether Π factors into per-site operators (Π = Π_A ⊗ Π_B).

**Result:** For every two-term Pauli coupling whose spectrum is palindromic, Π can be
written as a product of per-site maps, so the mirror is local. For 34 of 36 combinations
the per-site map is a discrete signed permutation (a Pauli relabelling with phases). For
exactly two, XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY, no discrete permutation works, but a single continuous
per-site unitary M does: M² = −I, the same on every site, with closed form given below.
These two were previously reported as non-local (operator Schmidt rank 8-9); that rank was
a non-convex search floor in the space of valid Π operators, not the family minimum, which
is rank 1. The non-locality lived in the choice of representative, not in the symmetry.

---

## 1. Why This Question Matters

The palindromic symmetry pairs every Liouvillian eigenmode with a partner at −λ − 2Σγ. If
this symmetry is a per-site property (Π = Π_A ⊗ Π_B), then the palindrome can be checked
and exploited locally, palindrome-breaking shows up in per-site syndromes, and the
structure is not topologically protected. If Π were irreducibly non-local, detecting the
break would need joint measurements, error correction would need entangled stabilizers,
and a topological invariant might protect the palindrome. Knowing which case holds tells
us what the palindrome is good for in practice. The answer, now uniform across all
palindromic cases, is the local one.

## 2. Setup and Method

### Notation

"XZ" denotes the Hamiltonian coupling term σ_X ⊗ σ_Z (Pauli X on site 0 tensored with
Pauli Z on site 1). "XZ+YZ" means H = J(σ_X ⊗ σ_Z + σ_Y ⊗ σ_Z), where both X and Y act on
site 0 and Z acts on site 1.

### Operator Schmidt decomposition

(The Schmidt decomposition is the standard test for entanglement: write the object as a sum
of tensor products and count how many terms are needed. One term means separable; more
means entangled.)

Π is a matrix on Liouville space (dimension d² = 16 for 2 qubits), which carries a
tensor-product structure C⁴_A ⊗ C⁴_B, one C⁴ per qubit's operator space (spanned by
I, X, Y, Z). To check whether Π factorizes, reshape it into a matrix whose rows index
(A-output, A-input) and whose columns index (B-output, B-input); its singular values are
the operator Schmidt coefficients. One nonzero singular value (rank 1) means Π is a
product; more means it is entangled in the chosen representative.

### Testing strategy and its widening

Per-site Π maps come in families known from
[Non-Heisenberg Palindrome](NON_HEISENBERG_PALINDROME.md):
- **P1:** swaps I↔X and Y↔Z (with phases), the X-channel crossover
- **P4:** swaps I↔Y and X↔Z (with phases), the Y-channel crossover

The original search tested products built from these *discrete* maps (P1⊗P1, P4⊗P4, P1⊗P4,
P4⊗P1, the alternating M2) and, where all failed, constructed Π numerically by eigenvalue
pairing. Two widenings matter. The first, already in the March analysis, was to allow
**non-uniform** discrete products (a different permutation per site); that resolved XZ+ZY
and YZ+ZX, where the X and Y demands sit on different sites. The second, the correction of
2026-06-02, is to allow a **continuous** per-site map: not a Pauli permutation but a
general block rotation. That resolves the last two, XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY, where both demands
sit on the same site. With this widening the per-site search is complete, and every
palindromic case is local.

## 3. Results

### Local via a discrete per-site permutation (rank 1)

**Uniform product (same map on all sites):**

| Hamiltonian | N | Π family | Conjugation error | Rank |
|-------------|---|-----------|-------------------|------|
| XX+YY+ZZ (Heisenberg) | 2 | P1⊗P1 | 1.1e-17 | 1 |
| ZZ (Ising) | 2 | P1⊗P1 | 2.0e-17 | 1 |
| XX | 2 | P1⊗P1 | 2.0e-17 | 1 |
| XX+YY+ZZ chain | 3 | P1⊗P1⊗P1 | 2.1e-17 | 1 |
| ZZ chain | 3 | P1⊗P1⊗P1 | 3.6e-17 | 1 |

**Non-uniform product (different maps per site):**

| Hamiltonian | N | Product Π | Conjugation error | Rank |
|-------------|---|------------|-------------------|------|
| XY | 2 | P1⊗M2 | 2.0e-17 | 1 |
| XZ+ZY | 2 | P4⊗P1 | 1.4e-17 | 1 |
| YZ+ZX | 2 | P1⊗P4 | 1.4e-17 | 1 |

For XZ+ZY: X appears on site 0, Y on site 1. Site 0 uses P4 (handles X-routing), site 1
uses P1 (handles Y-routing). No conflict, because the incompatible requirements sit on
different sites.

### Local via a continuous per-site rotation (rank 1): the corrected cases

For XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY, both X and Y land on the same site, and no single discrete crossover
routes both. A continuous per-site unitary does. The same map M works for both cases and on
every site, so Π = M^⊗N. In the framework Pauli order [I, X, Z, Y] its action is

    I ↦ −(X + Y)/√2        X ↦ (I + iZ)/√2
    Z ↦  i(X − Y)/√2       Y ↦ (I − iZ)/√2

with M unitary and M² = −I (an order-4 element, eigenvalues ±i).

| Hamiltonian | N | Product Π | Conjugation error | Rank |
|-------------|---|------------|-------------------|------|
| XZ+YZ | 2 | M⊗M | 2.2e-31 | 1 |
| XZ+YZ | 4 | M^⊗4 | 1.2e-14 | 1 |
| XZ+YZ | 6 | M^⊗6 | 7.7e-14 | 1 |
| ZX+ZY | 2..6 | M^⊗N | ≤ 7.4e-14 | 1 |

The residual is pure floating-point accumulation (M is exactly conditioned, cond = 1), so
the conjugation equation Π L Π⁻¹ = −L − 2Σγ·I holds exactly. These two are local.

### The earlier non-local reading, and where it came from

The March analysis reported XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY as non-local with minimum operator Schmidt
rank 9, "ranks 2 through 8 unreachable." Two things produced that reading. First, the
per-site search ranged only over discrete permutations, which genuinely fail here (the best
discrete product residual is 13.9 at N=2, growing to 96 at N=4). Second, where the discrete
search failed, Π was built by pairing Liouvillian eigenvectors, and that construction
returns an entangled representative under spectral degeneracy even when a product exists
(see §5, which already stated this). The rank-9 figure was the floor of a non-convex
minimization over the valid-Π space, not the family minimum; the family minimum is rank 1,
realized by M^⊗N above. The evidence was in fact already in the repository: section 4 of
`continuous_pi_search.py` ("full block optimization, 16 real parameters") found a per-site
continuous Π at 4.4e-12 for both cases. The narrative of the time followed section 5, the
eigenvector construction, and the section-4 product was set aside.

## 4. The Signal Routing Mechanism

### DC/AC bus structure

Z-dephasing splits each qubit's Pauli basis into two buses:
- **DC bus** {I, Z}: immune to noise (populations, decided outcomes)
- **AC bus** {X, Y}: decaying under noise (coherences, undecided)

Π is a crossover switch that swaps DC and AC. The discrete settings are P1 (routes the
X-channel) and P4 (routes the Y-channel); neither anticommutes with the Z-channel's adjoint
action, because the dephasing terms themselves provide the DC-bus impedance match.

### The diplexer conflict, and its continuous resolution

For XZ+YZ (X and Y on the same site), site 0 must route both the X-channel and the
Y-channel at once. P1 routes X but fails Y; P4 routes Y but fails X. No discrete setting
handles both, exactly the frequency conflict in a diplexer where two bands want
incompatible filter settings on one port. The resolution is not to add more ports but to
turn the filter: the continuous map M is the crossover rotated to the symmetric point
between P1 and P4, where it serves both bands at once. The rotation shows up directly in the
closed form, M sending the light pair to (X ± Y)/√2, the 45° combination of the two
channels. That the same combination, X + Y, is the bond's own operator (XZ + YZ = (X + Y)·Z)
is why one map suffices: the mirror rotates to face the very direction the coupling points.

### Why this was read as "minimum coupling depth 9"

A minimization that searches the full valid-Π space for the least-entangled representative,
rather than asking the cleaner yes/no question "does any product Π satisfy the conjugation
equation," lands on whatever local minimum the optimizer reaches. Here that was rank 9. The
product solution (rank 1) sits elsewhere in the same space and is found directly once the
per-site map is allowed to be continuous, or once M^⊗N is simply tested against the
conjugation equation. There is no multi-mode MIMO structure; there is a single rotated
crossover.

## 5. The Space of Valid Π Operators

Π is not unique. Any operator Π₀ S, where S commutes with L (a symmetry of the
Liouvillian), is also valid; the space of valid operators is isomorphic to the commutant of
L (all operators that commute with the Liouvillian).

| Hamiltonian | Commutant dim. | Distinct evals | Local? |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| Heisenberg | 44 | 7 of 16 | yes (P1⊗P1) |
| Ising ZZ | 64 | 4 of 16 | yes (P1⊗P1) |
| XX | 40 | 7 of 16 | yes (P1⊗P1) |
| XY | 40 | 7 of 16 | yes (P1⊗M2) |
| XZ+ZY | 36 | 9 of 16 | yes (P4⊗P1) |
| XZ+YZ | 40 | 9 of 16 | yes (continuous M⊗M) |

More eigenvalue degeneracy means a larger commutant and more freedom in choosing Π. Even
the smallest commutant here (dimension 36) is vast. This is why numerical construction from
eigenvector pairing always produces an entangled Π, even when a product solution exists, and
why it is most misleading exactly where the spectrum is most degenerate. The physically
meaningful question is not "is the numerical Π entangled?" but "does a product Π exist
anywhere in the valid space?" For XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY the answer is yes: the rank-1 product
M^⊗N. The degeneracy is heaviest at odd N (N=3 is a doubly special point, where the chain
also coincides with the star and ring by graph isomorphism), so the eigenvector route was
least trustworthy precisely at the small N first inspected; testing M^⊗N directly against
the conjugation equation sidesteps the eigenvector pairing entirely and is degeneracy-proof.

## 6. Implications

- **Error correction:** For every palindromic case, palindrome-breaking can be detected
  with local measurements (per-site syndromes). No case requires entangled stabilizers.

- **Topological protection (Q2):** No two-term case carries a topological invariant from
  mirror non-locality, because the mirror is local in all of them. The palindrome is a
  per-site property throughout.

- **Hidden symmetry Q:** The parity-breaking palindrome-preservers
  ([the other side](../hypotheses/THE_OTHER_SIDE.md)) carry a hidden Q ≠ Π. For all of them,
  including XZ+YZ and ZX+ZY, Q is a product operator: a per-site permutation for most, a
  per-site continuous rotation for the crossover pair.

- **What stays non-local is the break, not the mirror:** the 14 cases that lose the
  palindrome at N ≥ 3 do so through a genuine many-body conflict of adjacent bonds at the
  shared site (the V-Effect). That phenomenon is untouched by this correction; it concerns
  the absence of any Π, not the factorizability of an existing one.
