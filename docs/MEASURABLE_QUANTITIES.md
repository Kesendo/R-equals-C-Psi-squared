## The Core Formula Remains

```
R = CΨ²
```

What changes: We now attempt to assign measurable quantities to the variables.

## The Central Insight

**Reality = Stability**

Not "reality as a thing that exists." Reality as that which holds.

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Ψ | All possibilities, stable and unstable |
| C | The observing, the mirroring |
| R | What remains. What holds. What is stable. |

Ψ is large, perhaps infinite. But R is filtered. The process of mirroring (C) selects. Only what is stable remains.

This is why discrete energy levels in atoms. Why not all bonds are possible. Why structure instead of chaos.

## C as Completeness of Mirroring

### Definition

```
C = (Number of complete pairs) / (Number of possible pairs)
```

C is a value between 0 and 1.

| C | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| 0 | No mirroring, no stability |
| 0.5 | Half mirrored, partially stable |
| 1 | Completely mirrored, maximum stability |

### Examples: Electrons in Atoms

| Element | Electrons | Complete Pairs | Possible Pairs | C |
|---------|-----------|----------------|----------------|---|
| Hydrogen | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| Helium | 2 | 1 | 1 | 1 |
| Lithium | 3 | 1 | 2 | 0.5 |
| Beryllium | 4 | 2 | 2 | 1 |
| Neon | 10 | 5 | 5 | 1 |
| Sodium | 11 | 5 | 6 | 0.83 |

### This Explains

- **Noble gases don't react:** C = 1, nothing to gain
- **Alkali metals are reactive:** Unpaired electron, C < 1, seeks partner
- **Halogens are reactive:** One electron short of C = 1, seeks partner

## R as Binding Energy

### Definition

R ~ Binding Energy

The energy required to destroy a system. The higher, the more stable, the more "real."

### Examples: Atoms

| Atom | Ionization Energy (eV) | Interpretation |
|------|------------------------|----------------|
| Hydrogen | 13.6 | Baseline stability |
| Helium | 24.6 | Very high (C = 1) |
| Lithium | 5.4 | Lower (C = 0.5) |
| Neon | 21.6 | Very high (C = 1) |
| Sodium | 5.1 | Lower (C < 1) |

**Pattern:** High ionization energy correlates with high C.

### Examples: Atomic Nuclei

| Nuclide | Binding Energy per Nucleon (MeV) | Note |
|---------|----------------------------------|------|
| Iron-56 | 8.79 | Maximum, most stable nucleus |
| Nickel-62 | 8.76 | Near maximum |
| Helium-4 | 7.07 | Doubly magic |
| Uranium-238 | 7.57 | Heavy, unstable |

**Iron-56 has the highest R per particle of all atomic nuclei.**

This is why fusion ends at iron. Why fission ends at iron. Everything tends toward the maximum.

## Magic Numbers: Complete Mirroring in the Nucleus

Not only electrons have shells. Protons and neutrons do too.

**Magic numbers: 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126**

Nuclei with these numbers are especially stable.

| Magic Number | Element (Protons) | Meaning |
|--------------|-------------------|---------|
| 2 | Helium | First complete shell |
| 8 | Oxygen | Second complete shell |
| 20 | Calcium | Third complete shell |
| **28** | **Nickel** | Fourth complete shell |
| 50 | Tin | Fifth complete shell |
| 82 | Lead | Sixth complete shell |

### Doubly Magic

Some nuclei have magic numbers for protons AND neutrons:

| Nuclide | Protons | Neutrons | Stability |
|---------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Helium-4 | 2 | 2 | Extremely stable |
| Oxygen-16 | 8 | 8 | Very stable |
| Calcium-40 | 20 | 20 | Very stable |
| Lead-208 | 82 | 126 | Heaviest stable nuclide |

**Doubly magic = doubly mirrored = maximum R**

## Molecular Formation: The Rule

### When Bonds Form

```
Bond forms when: R_together > Σ R_separate
```

Systems seek maximum R. Maximum stability.

### Example: Hydrogen

| System | C | R | Status |
|--------|---|---|--------|
| H (single) | 0 | low | Reactive, unpaired |
| H + H → H₂ | 1 | 4.52 eV | Stable, paired |

Two incomplete mirrors find each other. C rises. R rises. The molecule exists.

### Example: Helium

| System | C | R | Status |
|--------|---|---|--------|
| He (single) | 1 | high | Already complete |
| He + He → He₂ | ≤1 | lower | No advantage |

Helium is already completely mirrored. Nothing to gain. No molecule.
