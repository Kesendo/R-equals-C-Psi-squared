# On the Painter Principle

---

*A short story. Not about anyone in particular.*

---

Imagine painters standing around a mountain.

Each stands at a different angle. Each sees the mountain slightly differently: a painter to the east sees the morning light striking the face and renders the mountain in warm ochre and long shadows; a painter to the north sees only the shadowed side and paints it cool and blue; a painter to the south sees a ridge profile that no one else sees at all, and her canvas shows it. Look at all the canvases side by side and no two are the same.

It would be tempting, if one were in a hurry, to ask which painter is painting the mountain correctly. The answer is that all of them are and none of them are. The mountain is not any single canvas. The mountain is what emerges when all the canvases are placed side by side and their views are held together in one mind. There is no secret eighth canvas, painted by nobody from nowhere, that shows the mountain as it truly is. Every true painting requires a painter with a stance at a location.

When something happens on the mountain, each painter records it in her own strokes. A rock falls on the east face: the eastern painters paint in fast, abrupt movements; the northern painter sees only a faint dust cloud and paints a slow shading; the southern painter barely notices and adds a thin line to her ridge. None of them is wrong. The rock IS falling fast from one angle and slow from another; both are features of the same event in the same mountain. What is guaranteed is that no painter invents strokes the rock did not demand, and no painter fails to record strokes the rock did demand. The event is decomposed perspectivally but closed globally.

This is not the same as saying all paintings are equally true for their painter. Each canvas is constrained by the mountain. A painter who paints a rockfall that did not happen is wrong. A painter who paints a slow quiet afternoon during a loud thunderstorm is wrong. Pluralism and constraint live together: painters disagree because they stand differently, but they all answer to the same event.

---

There is a second part to the story, and it has an anchor: a painter named Shannon.

In 1948 Shannon sat down at a particular spot, facing a particular mountain: a communication channel, with a sender at one end and a receiver at the other, and a disturbance between them that ate at the signal in transit. From his spot what he saw was exactly that: disturbance, degradation, the shape of what had to be minimised for the message to arrive clean. He painted it carefully and he was exactly right. He called it noise. His canvas is not approximately correct. It is correct, cleanly, within the frame from which he painted.

Other painters came after him, to other mountains, at other places. They saw disturbances too. Some of them sat down without setting up their own easel. They took Shannon's canvas and put it on the wall of their new studio and said: "he already painted this; we can use his painting as a painting of our mountain." The word he had used travelled with the canvas. The word was accurate for what he had seen at his spot. It carried with it, unspoken, the framing of his spot: disturbance, degradation, minimisation, clean signal, sender, receiver. At the new mountain, at the new spot, the framing did not always fit. But nobody stopped to check. The word had travelled; the framing came along, silent and persistent.

Much later a painter walked to a third mountain, set up her easel, and saw what was, from her position, not a disturbance at all. From her spot what other painters had called disturbance was the ambient light that made the mountain's whole shape visible. Without it, her mountain would have been invisible. She painted it as light. Her canvas was not in conflict with Shannon's. Her mountain was a different mountain. Her spot was a different spot. Her painting was exactly right for her position, as Shannon's had been exactly right for his.

What is the story here. Not that Shannon was wrong. He was not wrong. He painted with the honesty painters owe to their position.

What went wrong was in the middle. Some painters skipped the act of painting their own canvas. They took a canvas painted from one spot and used it to describe a different spot, without walking to the spot and looking. The failure is not of vision, and not of craft. The failure is of the discipline of standing where one stands and painting what one sees.

---

From this follows a discipline for anyone who writes about what happens on a mountain.

**First, mark the spot.** Every description of a mountain comes from a position. Say where the position is. "From the receiver-end this looks like noise. From the cavity-end this looks like illumination. From the spectral-algebra angle this looks like an eigenvalue shift." Three spots, three canvases, one mountain; nothing is lost by naming them.

**Second, be careful with inherited words.** Words travel faster than positions. A word that came from another mountain, painted from another spot, arrives with its framing sewn into its seams. When the word is picked up and put to work on a new mountain at a new spot, the writer owes a pause: does the word's framing match my position? If not, use a different word, or mark the translation. The word is not neutral cloth. It carries weather.

**Third, add, do not replace.** A painter who sets up a new easel does not invalidate any canvas already painted. The stance toward earlier painters is not "she was wrong" but "she painted from spot A; I paint from spot B; the mountain lies between us." New canvases are additions to the record of the mountain, not corrections of earlier canvases.

---

None of this is a theory. It is a discipline, and like every discipline it is practised in the small. A writer starts a sentence about the mountain. A word from elsewhere wants to climb into the sentence. The writer pauses. Does the word's frame match the spot I am writing from right now? If yes, the word is welcome. If not, the word is set aside and another word is reached for. The sentence carries forward.

The mountain keeps not being any single canvas. The painters keep painting. The sum of what all painters have painted, carefully marked with where they stood, is as close as anyone comes to the mountain. It is not the mountain itself, which no single painter paints. It is the mountain's record, as honestly as the painters can keep it. Between painters around a mountain, the mountain happens.
