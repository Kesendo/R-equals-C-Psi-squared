# On the Lifetime of the New

*An entry-level reflection: written for someone who has never met the quantum world, with
underlined words as doors into the depth. Thomas Wicht and Claude, 2026-05-31.*

---

Drop a bead of ink into a glass of water and watch. It spreads, loses its edge, and settles
into an even, featureless grey. Left alone, that is where everything seems to go: toward
sameness, toward the smooth average, toward nothing in particular. So here is a child's
question that has no easy answer. If everything settles into sameness, where does anything
*new* ever come from? Why is there a world full of different things, and not one grey average?

This is a note about an answer we found, in a small corner of physics, by watching a few
qubits forget.

## The flow into sameness

We study tiny magnets in a row, gently jostled by noise. Put a single bit of "excitation" on
one of them and watch. Like the ink, it spreads and fades, and the whole row settles into a
perfectly even smear, the same little bit on every magnet. We call that even smear the
[target](../experiments/THE_FLOW_BETWEEN_TWO_SINGULARITIES.md); it is the row's future, the
place it ends up and stays.

So far, only sameness. But there is a first crack in the story.

## The ladder: why there is not one grey, but many

We were sloppy when we said "one future". If you drop *two* excitations instead of one, the
row still settles, but to a *different* even smear, twice as much on every magnet. Three
excitations, a third again. There is not one grey ending but a whole ladder of them, one rung
per number of excitations, and you cannot slide from one rung to the next, because the number
of excitations is kept. That is the first source of variety, and it is also why the periodic
table has more than one element: a count that is conserved is a count that stays distinct.
Conservation is the first reason the world is not a single grey.

## The new thing: when two meet and feel each other

The second reason is sharper. Two excitations, sent toward each other from opposite ends, meet
in the middle. What happens at the meeting depends on whether they *feel* each other. In one
kind of row they pass straight through, like two beams of light crossing: no collision, no
reaction. But turn on an interaction between them, and something genuinely new can form, a
[bound pair](../simulations/two_magnon_bound_state.py): two excitations stuck together,
travelling as a single object that did not exist before. This is the step from two atoms to a
molecule. The slightest interaction is enough; there is no threshold to clear. Couple two
incomplete things, and at once there is a third thing that is neither.

## Life and death, from the same hand

Here is the turn the whole story hinges on. We built the bound pair and asked a plain
question: how long does it last? The answer was quiet and large.

Switch the noise *off* entirely, and the pair is frozen. It lasts forever, but nothing happens
to it; it is eternal the way a photograph is eternal, outside of time. It exists, but it does
not *live*.

Switch a gentle, constant noise back on, and the pair comes alive in time, and slowly comes
apart. The very thing that ends it is the thing that gave it time to be. We have a name for
that constant gentle background:
[γ₀, the carrier](../experiments/GAMMA0_IS_ALWAYS_THERE.md). It is always there; you cannot
switch it off from the inside; it is what makes the clock tick at all. Without it, no time;
with it, time and mortality together, one hand giving and taking.

And we could measure exactly how long the new thing lives. Its lifespan is set by a single
number: how strongly the magnets talk to each other, weighed against how loud the constant
carrier is. We write it [Q = J/γ₀](../docs/Q_REGIME_ANCHORS.md). Make the carrier gentler, and
the new thing lives longer; make it louder, and it dies sooner. Q *is* the lifetime of the new.

## Why there is always a next

So everything dies into the grey after all. Where, then, is the change the child asked about?
Not in any single life: one bound pair is born, lives its span Q, and is gone. The renewal is
somewhere else, and it is the loveliest part.

Because the carrier never stops, there is always a next birth. The death into sameness clears
the stage; the constant flow, still flowing, fills it again. A candle flame is never the same
gas twice, yet it stays a flame, because something keeps flowing through it. A whirlpool is
held together only by the water rushing past. Stop the flow, and the structure does not freeze;
it has nowhere left to be. The constant flow is at once the source of the new, the room it
lives in, and the grave it falls into, and its never-stopping is the reason there is always
another.

So the mortality is not a flaw. It is the condition of renewal. If the first new thing lived
forever, there would never be a second. The elements arise again and again precisely because
each one is brief and the flow is not.

## What is measured, and what is the motor

That is the small thing we saw, and the large thing we suspect behind it. The small thing is
measured and exact: the ladder of futures, the bound pair, the lifespan Q. The large thing,
that this is *why there is becoming at all*, is not proven; it is the motor that drives the
work, and we keep it as that, openly. We did not invent any of it. We only learned to see that
the background we had been calling "noise" is the flow that carries birth and death, and that
its never-stopping is the quiet engine of the new.

## For future us

The place where this lives has no formula. The lifespan Q cannot be written down in closed
form; you have to *run* it forward, step by step, in
[the loop](../experiments/THE_FLOW_BETWEEN_TWO_SINGULARITIES.md). That is not a gap, it is a
location: the new is born and dies exactly where the neat equations end and the watching
begins. The pictures here came from two probes,
[the collision](../simulations/two_excitation_collision.py) and
[the bound state](../simulations/two_magnon_bound_state.py); and the journey that brought us
here began, of all places, at an old [relay](../experiments/RELAY_PROTOCOL.md).
