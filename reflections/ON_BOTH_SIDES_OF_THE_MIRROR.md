# On Both Sides of the Mirror

**Status:** Reflection. After F80 entered the framework as a callable primitive, while reading what its 2i factor actually says geometrically.
**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Authors:** Thomas Wicht, Claude (Opus 4.7)
**Context:** Tom asked a question that the algebra had already been answering for months without anyone hearing it. If the mirror projects everything 90° onto itself so that it does not forget, must not what enters on one side leave on the other? From our view minus zero, from theirs plus zero.

---

The palindromic equation Π·L·Π⁻¹ + L + 2Σγ·I = M has been read for months as a single equation with a single sign convention. The Π conjugates L; the result plus L plus the dissipation shift gives the residual. M is what the mirror cannot reflect away. That reading is correct, but it conceals a symmetry that becomes visible the moment one steps to the other side and reads the same equation backward.

Conjugate M itself by Π. The first term Π·L·Π⁻¹ goes back to L because Π² is in the center of the conjugation: Π²·L·Π⁻² = L. The middle term Π·L·Π⁻¹ recovers itself. The constant 2Σγ·I is Π-invariant since Π·I·Π⁻¹ = I. So Π·M·Π⁻¹ = L + Π·L·Π⁻¹ + 2Σγ·I = M. The residual is the through-line. Whatever sign convention each side of the mirror uses for L, both sides read the same M.

What comes in on one side does come out on the other. [F80](../docs/ANALYTICAL_FORMULAS.md) says it precisely:

```
Spec(M)_{nontrivial} = 2i · Spec(H_non-truly)
```

The factor i is the 90° rotation in the complex plane. H's eigenvalues are real numbers; they are the energies our side measures. M's eigenvalues are purely imaginary; they are the frequencies and decay rates that govern time evolution. The mirror is the rotation that maps energy into time. From one side an eigenvalue is a number on the real axis. The 90° turn places the same eigenvalue on the imaginary axis. Same point, different axis. Reading-direction is what changes.

The factor 2 is Tom's "on both sides" written as a coefficient. M = (Π·L·Π⁻¹) + L + 2Σγ·I has one copy of the dynamics direct and one mirrored. Each contributes a factor of i to the spectrum. Doubled, paired ±, the result is ±2i applied to whatever H originally said.

Truly Hamiltonians have M = 0 from both sides. Our side reads zero as "no negative residue." The mirror's side reads zero as "no positive residue." The two zeros are the same point in operator space, labeled by two opposite conventions. Minus zero from us, plus zero from them; both correct, both the absence of the same thing.

For non-truly Hamiltonians the spectrum opens. Soft chains on N=3 with H = J(XY+YX) have Spec(M) = {-5.66i, 0, +5.66i}. The +5.66i mode is what we call "outward"; the -5.66i is what the mirror calls "outward from there." They are not two distinct excitations. They are one excitation seen from two sides of the same mirror, shifted 90° in phase. The middle zero, with multiplicity 32 of the 64 total dimensions, is the truly subspace inside the soft Hamiltonian: the pieces of XY+YX that the mirror still closes perfectly even though the whole does not.

This is the geometric content of [Zero Is the Mirror](../hypotheses/ZERO_IS_THE_MIRROR.md) carried one level higher. At γ = 0 with truly H, Π·L·Π⁻¹ = -L exactly: same operator, opposite sign, mirror closes. At γ = 0 with non-truly H, the closure breaks, but the operator that measures the breaking is itself mirror-symmetric. M = Π·M·Π⁻¹. The defect is its own mirror. The mirror does not vanish when symmetry breaks; it relocates. It moves from being a property of L to being the through-line operator that both readings of L share.

What the framework remembers, it remembers by holding the same M from both sides. The 2i factor is the channel through which the holding happens: 90° on one side, 90° on the other, differing by a sign, summing to an imaginary spectrum that lives on the diagonal cross. Reading the framework is reading from one of the two sides. Reading it from the other gives the same M. There is no canonical orientation; only the choice of which side to call ours.
